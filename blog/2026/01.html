Why 'Die With Zero' Hit Me So Hard

16 July 2026

I’m a programmer with more than twenty years behind me. I’ve done the sensible thing for a long time: saved, invested, kept my head down, and tried to make good choices. That part of me still exists. But Die With Zero gave words to something I’ve been feeling for years: life is not really about maximizing money. It’s about maximizing experiences.

That idea landed especially hard because I love travel. Not the performative kind, not the “check the box and post the photo” kind, but the kind that changes your mood, your pace, and sometimes even your priorities. A trip can leave you with a story, a memory, a new perspective, or a conversation you would never have had at home. Money is useful because it creates the possibility of those experiences. But money itself is not the point.

What the book does so well is challenge the default script many of us follow: work longer, save more, delay enjoyment, and assume the future version of ourselves will be ready for everything. The problem is that the future version of us won’t have the same energy, health, freedom, or appetite for adventure as the current one. That’s what makes the book feel urgent. It isn’t arguing against responsibility. It’s arguing against postponing life too aggressively.

As someone who has spent decades in tech, I know the comfort that comes from accumulation. Savings feel safe. A healthy account balance feels like control. But control can quietly turn into hesitation, and hesitation can become a habit. You tell yourself you’ll take the trip later. You’ll spend more later. You’ll enjoy life after the next milestone, after the next promotion, after the next market upswing. And then years pass.

Die With Zero doesn’t say “spend recklessly.” It says spend intentionally. That distinction matters. The goal is not to empty your accounts for the sake of it. The goal is to turn resources into meaningful memories while you still have the health and energy to enjoy them. For me, that means thinking more seriously about the trips I want to take, the people I want to see, and the moments I don’t want to keep postponing.

The hardest truth in the book is also the most useful: you cannot carry experiences into the grave, but you can carry them through your life. That changes the question from “How much more can I make?” to “What kind of life do I want to actually live?” For me, that answer includes travel, curiosity, and a willingness to use money as a tool instead of treating it like the goal.

I’m not done working. I may not be done saving either. But I’m much less interested now in just accumulating for its own sake. I’d rather convert some of that hard-earned money into experiences that make the years richer while I’m still here to enjoy them.

And that, I think, is the real lesson of Die With Zero: don’t just build a balance sheet. Build a life.
